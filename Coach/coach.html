Coach Mobile App

Overview

Summer 2019, my team of six ranked 3rd in a Tapestry Case Study Competition, where we designed a mobile app for the luxury brand, Coach.

Roles: UX/UI Designer, UX Researcher

Date: June 24th - July 30th, 2019

Tools: Sketch, Adobe Photoshop

The Challenge

Coach wanted to connect to their customers in a new, innovative way. How could we design a mobile app that would appeal to all demographics, especially Gen Z, while maintaining Coach's values and brand?

Coach.com was seeing a huge jump in the number of mobile visitors. There was also a huge increase in conversion rates through mobile. As a result, Coach was exploring the introduction of a mobile app for consumers to use at home and potentially in stores to enhance the Coach shopping experience. Many of their rival companies had developed mobile apps to capitalize on convenience and open a new line of communication between customer and brand. Coach also faced difficulties connecting to younger generations and wanted to change that.

Research

Before working on the actual design, we needed to get an idea of what consumers are looking for. Our research found that Augmented/Virtual Reality was one of the big technological trends. The Gucci app, for example, uses AR to allow users to try on shoes and glasses. Through more research and interviewing Tapestry employees, we found these other key points:

1. 57% of customers are using mobile apps while in-store.

2. 48% of shoppers would be more likely to shop at a retailer that utilizes AR/VR.

3. Wish lists/favorites features is a major reason why people would prefer mobile app over website.

4. Gen Z is a rewards-driven group, with 83% showing greater loyalty to brands that provide rewards and surprises.

User Persona

Since the app would mainly target Gen Z, we developed a Gen Z persona to further help us understand what the app needed.



Ideation

Based off of our research, we decided to create an app that would:

1. Utilize AR/VR to allow users to quickly try on products in/out of stores.

2. Build an online community by letting users share/save photos/products and featuring social media influencers/Coach ambassadors.

3. Utilize a rewards system to incentivize users to keep using the app.

Once we formulated an idea, we created a flow chart to help us flesh out the details.



One of my teammates, Morgan Rossi, also drew a few amazing sketches to help think of the app's appearance!

For further inspiration, we visited the recently opened Coach store in Hudson Yards, NYC, since that's the store design Coach is pushing forward. There's a large pink room in the store, so we decided to make pink a motif in the app. The store also has a customization lab that features a neon sign on a metal fence, so we made that a motif as well.


High Fidelity Wireframes

These wireframes are redesigns of the ones submitted, following the judges' critiques that the social media platform felt a bit crowded. I was originally responsible for the home and shop pages and rewards system. These wireframes below are all by me.

The app icon. The goggles are the "C" from Coach. They're a nod to the VR aspect of the app and symbolize viewing the world through Coach's eyes. The background is a peach color that Coach commonly uses for their products.


The home page. Since this is the page users see first, we wanted it to get them excited about exploring the app. The top section would feature a new, exciting thing happening at Coach. In this wireframe, users can watch Coach's collection at NYFW live, only on the app. Below could be new arrivals and the latest posts from Coach's ambassadors. At the very top, users can search through the app and access their virtual fitting room and bag. The bottom nav bar shows the core sections.

To appeal to Gen Z, we created a social platform where people can share how they wear Coach and save posts made by others. Users can also follow Coach's ambassadors. The posts are listed in a blog-like layout, as opposed to a standard grid, to make the page more fun and interesting.

The shop page. To organize the products better, women's, men's, and sale products are divided up and everything can be sorted and filtered. Again, note the blog layout for the products. Users also have the option to save items they like.



The fitting room. The fitting room. When users first set up an account, they are asked to create an avatar, which they can edit anytime. The avatar can try on all the products to give users an idea what they would look like on them. We felt this feature was important because many people hesitate to buy products online out of fear that it would look bad on them.


To make the app usable in stores, we've included a camera where users can take photos of a product and try them on virtually. This would help users save time from waiting for an available fitting room and putting on/taking off items. They would also have the option to save or share the photo.


Your profile page is where you can edit your avatar and view your saved posts. You can also view your rewards. A rewards system would further motivate people to use the app. We created a rewards system where people can win Coach charms that are used for customization. The system is based off of Snapchat trophies. There are different sets of charms, where each charm builds off of each other. For example, to unlock the Black Tea Rose, users would need to spend over $100. To unlock the Blue Tea Rose, users would need to spend at $200, and to unlock the Purple Tea Rose, a $1000. Each reward would offer a discount or access to an event or sale. There's an expiration date for the rewards so that users can't stack too many discounts on top of each other and get items for free.

Feedback & Reflection

Responses from the judges were positive. They found our design to be creative and innovative. The one critique they had was our original social media platform felt a bit crowded. Corrections to this were made.

This was my first time designing a mobile app, and for a large company. I was pushed to think bigger and smaller at the same time: I had to come up with innovative ideas while keeping in mind that a phone screen is tiny. It was a new experience that helped me grow greatly. If I were to redo it, I would test the design with users to see how they respond.
