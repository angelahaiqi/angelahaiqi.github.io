Jennifer Sue Website Redesign

Overview

Jennifer Sue is a small fashion doll business that I worked with for for several years. I took the initiative to revamp its website, which was created with Yahoo Small Business.

Role: UX Designer, Web Designer

Date: November 2018

Tools: Yahoo Small Business, Adobe Photoshop

Background

Jennifer Sue is a small fashion doll business that was created in the early 2000s. They design dolls and doll clothing/accessories. Since I personally knew the owner, I worked part-time for them, first in sales and then product development. Jennifer Sue was successful in its early years, but overtime, sales began to decrease. I felt a major reason was its lack of proper digital outreach. Jennifer Sue was not on social media and its website had not been updated since 2012. I therefore took the initiative to create social media accounts and redesign the website.

The Challenge

Redesign the Jennifer Sue website so that it is more modern, visually pleasing, and user-friendly.

The Jennifer Sue website had not been updated for several years, and it showed with its old design. From going through it in-depth, I also noted some strange user experiences.

This is what the site initially looked like when you entered jenniferdolls.com. The customer is supposed to click on the product they're interested in, leading them to the product page. From there, they have access to the rest of the website (notably, the menu). The logic was to promote the newest products. The idea wasn't bad, but I felt the execution was potentially confusing, since it's not clear you're supposed to click on the images. Not showing the menu further removes clear navigation.

After clicking into one of the images, you have access to all the other pages in the website. This is the about page. It's typical for doll companies to create a biography for the doll. There's also a section about the doll's physical attributes.

Jennifer Sue in the news. I also felt this page could be cleaned up a bit. Also note one of the menu options: "Link." It did not lead to anywhere.

The Jennifer Sue dolls. As you can see, the description is stretched out a lot, making the page unnecessarily wide.

After taking note about issues with the website, I researched other doll companies. One of the biggest fashion doll companies is Integrity Toys.

Integrity Toys' site was simple yet sophisticated. It allowed customers to focus on their dolls while projecting the image that these dolls are high-end. I used this website along with other eCommerce sites as inspiration. The Jennifer Sue website was built with Yahoo Small Business, so I used that platform to redesign.

The Result

The new home page. Pink and black are Jennifer Sue's colors, hence the usage of them throughout the website. When I redesigned the website, I not only changed the appearance of the home page, but also included Jennifer Sue's biography (and also a description of the company itself). By giving the customer immediate access to some key information, this redesign reduces the amount of steps needed to learn more about Jennifer Sue and piques interest.

I included real reviews and news coverages on the home page as well to help build more trust in Jennifer Sue. Lastly, I added the contact information at the bottom of the page. Unfortunately, Small Business would not let me change the colors of the links, but at least it's clear they can be clicked on.

The about page. I decided to create a separate page for the founder's story and include a more in-depth description of the company, so that the user isn't overloaded with information on the home page. I removed "Jennifer's Journal," which was meant to be like a diary for the doll, because the company had stopped working on it for years and had no intention on reviving it.



The dolls page (first image) and the clothing page (second image). For the dolls, I used photos showing their full bodies instead of just their faces like in the previous design. I felt it was important to present the full body of the doll, as opposed to its face, so that customers have the best idea of what they're clicking into. Similarly, with other products such as clothing, I thought the photos should allow the customers to see the products clearly.



Individual doll page. I was able to create a cart with HTML, so I added an "add to bag" button that the original website did not have. Small Business does not offer much flexibility, so I needed to create an extra section for the button. I decided to use the extra space to include more photos. Other product pages are set up this way too.


And finally, I created a contact page so customers can directly reach out to Jennifer Sue!

Reflection

Jennifer Sue's brand awareness and sales increased after the website was redesigned and social media accounts were activated. The one problem I found with the website was its responsiveness. Small Business did not allow me to make mobile-only features or dimensions. I had to do my best to fit the product photos into the mobile frames provided and make sure the most important parts were not cut off. Had I found a way to make the mobile layout a little less awkward, I think our sales would increase even more.

This was the first project I took on that affected a real company. I had to learn to be strategic in my design, as opposed to just seeing if it looked pretty. Putting myself in the shoes of the customer taught me so much about UX and web design. Additionally, using Small Business tested my problem-solving skills. I had to think outside the box to get around Small Business' limitations. If I were to redo this project, I would test my design before releasing it (and find an alternative to Small Business).
